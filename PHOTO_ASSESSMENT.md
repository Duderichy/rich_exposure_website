## Overall Quality Assessment

**Your best work is genuinely excellent.** The Iceland series in particular — Skogafoss with the rainbow, the Dyrhólaey black sand cliffs at golden hour, the silhouetted figure on the black beach, the glacier lagoon — this is work that belongs in travel publications. It's not "good for a hobbyist" or "good for someone building a portfolio." It's good, full stop.

What separates strong work from average work at the professional level is usually three things: light awareness, compositional instinct, and post-processing taste. You demonstrate all three consistently.

### Strengths

**Light awareness is your biggest asset.** You consistently find and exploit the best available light. The golden hour Iceland landscapes, the blue hour Dublin Custom House, the warm amber/teal color palette in the cinematic portrait (DSC06861), the way you use harsh directional light in the Dublin street shots — this isn't luck. You're reading light well and positioning yourself accordingly.

**Color science and post-processing.** Your color grading is sophisticated and, more importantly, consistent within series. The Iceland edits have a cohesive teal/gold palette. The Dublin night work has a distinctive warmth. The B&W conversions (snow mountain, subway platform, Hallgrimskirkja) show restraint — you're not just desaturating, you're making tonal decisions. This is the kind of thing that separates a $400/session photographer from a $750+ one.

**Compositional range.** You're comfortable working at every focal length and every scale — from macro orchids to sweeping glacier panoramas. The Trinity College Long Room shot, the cypress tree tunnel, the subway stairwell shot (L1030725) where you've caught three people in a dynamic diagonal — these show an eye that adapts to the subject rather than imposing a formula.

**The ballet/dance series is outstanding.** DSC04448 (ballerina spinning on pointe), DSC04462 (synchronized backbend), DSC04480 (dynamic floor work) — this is legitimately professional-grade performance photography. The B&W treatment is consistent, the timing is precise, and you're capturing peak action with intention. This series alone could anchor a portfolio section and attract performing arts clients.

**Multi-system fluency.** You shoot Sony, Leica, and Ricoh GR. The Leica street work has a distinctive rendering that you're clearly exploiting intentionally (the NYC subway/street shots have a different character than the Sony work). This isn't just gear collecting — you're matching the tool to the situation.

### Areas for Development

**Wedding portfolio gap.** This is the most significant gap relative to your pricing. You're listing destination weddings at $12k and NYC weddings at $8.5k, but I found zero wedding images in your archives. The Iceland travel work, the portrait sessions, and the event coverage all suggest you could deliver excellent wedding work — but clients paying $8-12k+ need to see proof. Building this portfolio (even through styled shoots, second-shooting for established wedding photographers, or heavily discounted first weddings) should be the top business priority if weddings are a serious revenue target.

**Curation discipline.** The portrait projects folder has 323 frames from a single session. The some_iceland folder has 49 images. For personal archives this is fine, but for client delivery and portfolio presentation, tighter editing matters. The difference between "good photographer" and "photographer I'll pay premium rates for" often comes down to showing only the best 15-20 frames, not the best 50. Your portfolio_prints folder (10 images) shows you know how to curate — apply that same discipline everywhere.

**Signature style definition.** Your technical range is a strength, but it can also dilute brand identity. The best-paid photographers in any genre tend to have an immediately recognizable look. Your Iceland/travel work has the most cohesive identity right now — that moody, cinematic, warm-cool palette. Consider whether that visual identity can extend more deliberately across your portrait and street work to create a unified "Rich Exposure look."

**Portrait posing and direction.** The candid/documentary portraits (laughing man, cowboy boots skateboard, Dublin busker) are strong. The directed portrait work (Caleb autumn series, Camille headshots) is good technically but could push further creatively. The strongest directed portrait in the set is DSC06168 (mirror/lipstick) — it has narrative tension, environmental context, and intentional composition. More of that energy in portrait sessions would elevate the work.

## Business Plan Recommendations

### Year 1 Priority: Build the Wedding Portfolio

This is the single highest-leverage thing you can do. Weddings are the revenue engine for most photography businesses, and you're pricing into a bracket that demands proof.

**Action items:**
1. **Second-shoot for 3-5 established NYC wedding photographers.** Reach out to photographers in the $5-10k range. Offer your services as a second shooter at $500-$800/day. You'll build a wedding portfolio, learn the workflow, and make industry connections. Many top wedding photographers started exactly this way.
2. **Shoot 2-3 styled wedding shoots.** Collaborate with wedding planners, florists, and venues who also need portfolio content. Everyone benefits, and you get editorial-quality wedding images for your site.
3. **Offer 3-5 weddings at a discounted "portfolio-building" rate.** $3,000-$4,000 for a full wedding, explicitly framed as an introductory rate. This gets you real wedding experience and images. Raise to full pricing after you have the portfolio to back it up.

### Brand Strategy

**"Rich Exposure" is a strong name.** It's memorable, has a double meaning (rich imagery / luxury positioning), and works across genres.

**Lead with travel.** Your travel work is your strongest differentiator and the most visually striking content on the site. Most NYC wedding photographers don't have a travel portfolio like yours. This is your competitive advantage — position it as proof that you can capture beauty anywhere, which is exactly what destination wedding clients want to hear.

**Develop the dance/performance niche.** The ballet series is strong enough to market specifically to dance companies, performing arts schools, and theater productions. This is a less competitive niche with good rates ($1,500-$3,000 per performance/rehearsal shoot) and it generates portfolio content that's visually stunning.

**Social media strategy:** Post consistently (3-5x/week on Instagram), but curate ruthlessly. Only post your top 10% work. The temptation is to post everything — resist it. One incredible Iceland image gets more traction than five decent ones.

### Pricing Evolution

**Now:** Your current pricing is appropriate for your skill level as you build the commercial portfolio. Don't raise prices until demand forces you to.

**6-12 months:** Once you have 5+ weddings and 20+ portrait/headshot clients, raise headshots to $500, portraits to $900, events to $2,000.

**12-24 months:** With a proven wedding portfolio and steady bookings, raise NYC weddings to $10,000, destination to $15,000. This is where the real money is.

**The key metric:** When you're booking 80%+ of inquiries and your calendar is filling 3+ months out, it's time to raise prices. If you're booking less than 30% of inquiries, your portfolio needs work before prices go up.

You have the eye, the technical skill, and the post-processing sophistication to compete at premium rates in the NYC market. The gap isn't talent — it's portfolio breadth and market proof. The Iceland/Ireland travel work would not look out of place in Conde Nast Traveler. The street work has genuine voice. The portrait and dance work shows range.
